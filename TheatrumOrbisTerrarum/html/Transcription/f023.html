AFRICA This the Ancients have diuersly distinguished; but at this present it is divided by John Leo of Africa, into foure chiefe parts; Barbaria, Numidia, Libya, and the Land of Negros. Barbarie, which is accounted the best, they circumscribe with the Atlantick & Mediterranean seas; with mount Atlas, & whither region of Barca bordering upon Ægypt. Numidia, called by the inhabitants Biledulgerid, and abounding with Dates (for which cause the Arabians call it by no other name, but the Date-bearing region) is bounded Westward by the Atlantick Ocean; Northward by mount Atlas; it stretcheth East as farre as the citie Caphsa, which is an hundred & fiftie miles distant from Ægypt; and the sande Deserts of Libya embrace it on the South. Libya the third part, is named in the Arabian tongue Sura, which word signifies a Desert. It begins East from Nilus, and thence runneth West as farre as the Atlantick sea; Numidia lies to the North of it, and the Land of Negros to the South. Now followeth the fourth part, which they call Nigritarum or Nigritania, either from the inhabitants, who are of a blacke colour, or from the river Niger, that runneth thorow the countrey. It is bounded by Libya, South by the Æthiopick Ocean; West by Guinee, and East by the Kingdome of Gago. And here we are to note, that according unto their division, all Africa is included within the Mediterranean, Atlantick, and Æthiopick seas and the river Nilus; whereof Ægypt and Abassia are accounted parts of it, which we not unworthily therefore proper to belong to Africa. For the true Africa containes at this day Prester Johns Empire, which by all late Writers is ascribed to Africa. Wherefore with Ptolomy indeed it ought to be bound by the Mediterranean sea and Ocean seas, rather than by any river whatsoever; and it hath the forme of a Peninsula, being joined toward Asia by an Isthmos of small neck-land, which lies between the Mediterranean sea and the gulf of Arabia. The South part hereof was unknowne to our ancestors, till the yeere 1497, whereas Vasco de Gama first doubling the Cape of Bona Speranza, and passing about all Africa, came to Calecut in East India. This Southerne part by the Persians and Arabians is called Zangibar. At the fore-sayd Cape of good hope the inhabitants are exceeding blacke; which we thought in no wise to omit, because all men suppose the cause of blacknesse to be heat, and the nearnesse of the Sunne; whereas the Sunne of it selfe forceth no more, than about the Streight of Magellan (we measure the heat of the place, according to the position of the heavens and distance from the Equinoctiall line) were now notwithstanding the people are reported to be marvellous white. But if we will needs ascribe this blacknesse to the scorching of the Sun, let us consider, that the same makes the Spaniards and Italians looke white; whereas they are equally distant from the Equinoctiall with the inhabitants of the fore-sayd Cape; namely the one towards the South, and the other towards the North. Prester Johns people are of a browne colour; in Zanel and Mollala the inhabitants are cole blacke, yet all in one & the same distance from the equator, and under the very same parallell of the heavens. And on the contrary, why did Herodotus and Pindarus describe such as inhabite the same climate with themselves, namely Colches, to be a blacke people, and curled haired? Herodotus in his Thalia makes the Indians blacke like the Æthiopians; which the experience of our times confirmeth. I know Herodotus will have the cause hereof to be the seede of the parents, which he sayth is not whiter, than of other people, but blacke. To whom Possidonius also subscribeth, and imputeth the matter of all this blacknesse unto Canicular heate, against which opinion I have nothing to allege. Let the truth of the matter rest upon the authors credit. But this as many men thinke more strange; that in all America there were no black people found besides a few only in one place called by them Quarequa. What then is the efficient cause of this colour? Is it the drinesse of the heavens, or of the earth? is it perhaps some hidden propriety of the soile? Or a kind of fat licentious nature in the nature of men? Or is it all these variances in one? But these things we leave to be considered by them, who more curiously search into the secrets of nature. This portion of the world is called by the Greeks Libya, and by the Latines Africa, because it is not moistened with extremity of cold: or (if we may credit some) of Afer one of the adventurers posterity. Another derivation of the name you have (set downe by John Leo in the description of his booke of the Description of Africa), who deriveth it from one blacke African, springing of the stocke of Cham, who first inhabited that part of the Mediterranean sea, from Atlas, bounded by the river Nilus, or rather as the best authors have taught us, the African soile called of the Greekes Aphrice, Marce rubrum, and red sea. Herodotus reporteth, that Africa was first unknowne till Cesars time, when it was found to be a third part of the inhabited world. Plinie the like, and so doth Solinus. Caesar Scaliger gathereth of the word Africa, that it is a name of the same import with aprica, that is, a sunne-shining place; for that this land lying open on every side to the Sunnes visitation, seemed thereby to have beene named so little other than of apricity. The principall regions of Africa have amongst themselves (as it were) a threefold division: first, from Atlas in the West to the red sea and Arabia, whence it took the name of the first inhabiters, or else from the colour of the people, for the most part blacke and duskie; the second, to wit, the inner part of Africa, from Nilus to the Kingdome of Congo, is more temperate, and not so much subject to the Sunnes heate; the third, which is next unto the South Pole, by reason of the obliquity of the Zodiack, is more temperate than the former. The islands that belong to Africa (some of which are expressed in the table) are Madagascar, the Canaries, the Isles of Cape Verde, and St. Thomas situated under the equinoctiall, which aboundeth with fruits. Among the ancient Writers none have particularly described this region. But concerning the same saith Isidore in his Etymologies; Hanno his navigation about Africa, in diversities; and Iambulus his voyages, in Diodorus Siculus; and likewise Herodotus his Melpomene. Bartholomeus de Glanvilla in his Image of the World reporteth the like. Of late Writers reade also the Commentaries of Vasco de Gama, Franciscus Alvares, who travelled Æthiopia. But of all others John Leo hath most exactly described it; as also Andrew Thevet, and Francis Alvarez, both travellers of Africa. Concerning the river Nilus, the greatest in all the world, you have the letters of John Ramusio and Jerome Fracastorius. Of Africa itself, as by me read at large in the second volume of Mr. Richard Hakluyts English Voyages.
