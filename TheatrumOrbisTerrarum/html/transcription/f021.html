ASIA Asia diuides it selfe from Europe by the river Tanais, and a streight line drawen from the fountaines thereof to the Bay of S. Nicolas in the North Ocean: but from Africa, by the neck-land Ifthmos or narrow place of the earth, which in betweene the Arabian gulfe and the Mediterran sea. The residue thereof the Ocean and other seas do enuiron. This part the ancient Geographers, as Strabo in his eleuenth booke, and Ar- rianus in his fift booke, haue dinersly divided. But at this present it may be us not altogether un- fitly be distributed into fine parts, according to the great Empires by which it is gouerned. The first whereof, which bordereth upon Europe, and is subiect to the great Duke of Mofcouia, is bounded by the Frozen sea, the riuer Ob, the lake Kitaia, and a line drawen from thence to the Caspian sea, and by the Isthmus or neck-land between the Caspian and Euxin seas. The second is that, which yields obedience to the great Cham Emperour of the Tartars, whole Southern borders are the Caspi- an sea, the riuer Iaxartest, and mount Imaus; on the East and North it is begirt with the Ocean; and Westward it confines vpon the dominions of the foresaid great Duke of Russia. The third part, the progeinie of the Ottomans or the Turkish Emperour enioyes, which containeth whatsoeuer re- gions are situate within the compasse of the Euxin sea, the aegean sea (now called Arcipelago) and the Mediterranean sea, the land of Egypt, the Arabian and Persian seas, the riuer Tigris, the Ca- spian sea, and the Isthmos or neck-land between it and the sea Euxine. Vnder the fourth shalbe com- prised the Kingdome of Persia, now gouerned by the Sophies; Westward it hat the Turkes, with whom the Persian for the most part manteineth continuall warres; Northward the Empire of the great Cham; Eastward it extends almost to the riuer Indus; and on the south it is restrained by the sea, at this present called Indicum, but in times past Mare Rubrum, or The Red sea. Now the sift part takes vp all that remaineth, which as in times past, so at this present, is called The Indies; neither is it gouerned by one only, as the former; but by many pety Kings, euery region almost hauing a peculiar Gouernour, some which are tributarie to the great Cham. And here it is not to be omitted, that all the sea-coasts from the Arabian gulfe, to the Promontory of China, commonly called Cabo de Lampo, situate in 30. degrees of Northerly latitude, are in a maner possessed, or made tributary to the Portugals. Amongst infitinte others, the princpall isles ascribed to Asia, are Cyprus and Rhodes in the Mediterran sea; Zeilan of the vulgar and Taprobana well knowento the ancients now called Sa- motra, but falsly Sumatra, in the Indick-Ocean, where also within the memory of our fathers, the Portugals haue discouered the two Iauas, with the isles of Borneo, Celebes, Palohan, andMin- danao; and here likewise Gilolo, and the Moluccos, at this day famous for abundance of spi- ces which they yeeld. To these may be added the isles of Iapan, and new Guinie very lately descried; but this last, whether it be an island, or adioyned to the South maine, it is not yet Certainly knowen. The memorie of this part of the world is most renowmed, not only with prophane authors (as they terme them) in regard of the first monarchies established by the Assyrians, the Persians, the Babylo- nians, and the Medes; but in the holy Scriptures also it is more famous than the rest: for here man- kinde was not only created by almightie God, deluded and corrupted by the enemie Satan, and resto- by our Saviour Christ; but we finde almost all the hestories in either Testament to haue beene in this part both written and accomplished. Strabo hath described Asia in six books, namely from the 11. booke vnto the 16. Ptolomey hath decyphered the fame in three, as in his 5. 6. And 7. books; and likewise in 12. Geographicall Tables, and ascribes unto it 40. prouinces. It is also let foorth by Ammianus Marcellinus in his 23. booke, and by Diodorus Siculus in the beginning of his 18. Of late Writers none haue wholly and particularly described it. M. Paulus Venetus, Lewes of Barthema, and St. Iohn Mandeuile, (but this Authour is fraught with tables) haue written so much thereof, as by trauelling the regions, they found worthie of obseruation. Reade also the Epistle of Iacobus Nauarca a Fesuite. I my selfe likewise certaine yeeres past set forth a large Map the description of the same.
