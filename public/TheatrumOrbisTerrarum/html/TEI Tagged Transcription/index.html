This is that which S. Paulinus in Aulonius, out o the opinion of the same Saluit, thus writeth of : Europam Afiamque duo vel maxima terrae Membra, quibus Lybiam dubie Sallustius addit, Europæ admixtram : poffit quum tertia dici : Europe and Asia vast, the Earthly globe between them share. Yet whether Africke should a part of this our Europe be ; or make a third part by it selfe, Saluit doth doubt I see. But Philostratus also in Isocrates doth diuide the world into Asia and Europe : yea Isocrates himself, in his Pane- gyricos. Moreouer in Varroes booke De lingua Latina these words are read ; As all the world is diuided into Heaven and Earth : so Heaven is seuered into his quarters : and the Earth into Asia and Europe. Againe the same author in his booke of Husbandrie writeth thus : first, when as the world, by Eratosthenes, was very fully and naturally divided into two parts, the one toward the South, [Asia doubtlesse he meaneth ] the other in the north [Europe we call it.] S. Augu- stine in his 16. booke De Ciuitate Dei : Lucane in his 9. booke: and Orosius in the first booke of his history, haue the like wordes to the same sense. Notwithstanding custome since hath preuailed with all historiographers and cosmographers which haue written either in Latine or Greeke, ioinly to diuide the globe of the earth into these three parts: Asia, Africke and Europe: the last of which we have taken vpon vs to describe in this place, not only in forme of a mappe or chart, like a Geographer: but in this present discourse, like an historian. Concerning the forme of it therefore, it is manifold, as Strabo writeth. It is a Peninsula or demy-isle, and not an island; although Silenus, as Elianus writeth, did sometime to Midas so relate of it. For it is on all sides, as you may see in the mappe, bounded and beaten with the salt sea, but only upon the East, where it is by a small necke ioined to the greater Asia. Yet by what limits they are there distinguished, the ancient and the later writers do not altogether agree. For those which are more ancient as Aristotle, Plato, Herodotus, and others which do follow their opinion, do diuide Europe from Asia, by the riuer Phasis, (a riuer of Colchis falling into the Euxine sea, Mar Maiore, or Maurothalassa, as the greeks call it) neere Trapezonda : some mappes do now call that riuer Fasso, others Phaz- zeth: the Scythians, (as Theuet reporteth, Debbaffethca) or which is all one, by that ithmos or neckland which is between the forefaid Mar Maiore or Pontus Euxinus, and the Caspian sea (Mar de Cachu, the ancient called it Mare Hyrcanum, the Hyrcanie sea) which formerly all old writers thought to be but a bay or gulfe of the Scythian or Northren ocean, as Strabo, Pliny, Mela, Dionysius, Plutarch (in the life of Alexander and in his discourse of the face in the sphere of the Moone) and Iornandes a more late writer, have first recorded. Yet all of them were deceiued. Only Herodotus truly (as this our latter age doth approve and find to be so) doth affirme this to be a sea of it selfe, and to have neither in-let nor out-let, or to be intermeddled with any other sea. Dionysius, Arrianus, Diodorus, Polybius, Iornandes and Ptolemey have diuided it from Asia by the riuer Tanais (Don or Tana, as now the Italians name it) who think that both the rise of this riuer, and the land Northward from where it commeth, are both unknown and uncertaine. All doubt where to place and lay their bounds : as indeed who never perfectly knew those places toward the East and North, not being then discovered, but only described by them, from the fabulous reports of others: as for example, the Riphæan and Hyperboræan mountaines, which are fained inventions of the Greekes, as Strabo writeth: together with Aluani montes, heere described by Ptolemey, where not only these mountaines, but also no other at this day, are to be seene: but in their places diuers huge and vast woods, great fennes and bogges, or large champion plaines. Orpheus also long since described in this part of the continent I meane, between Mæotis palus, the fenne Mæotis, (now called Mar delle Zabbache, and Mardella Tana) and the sea Cronium, an huge wood. Likewise, Dionysius after heereabouts placeth an infinite wood, as he termeth it, from whence he saith Tanais or Don doth spring, which after many windings and turnings at last falleth into the forenamed fenne Maeotis. Isidorus heere hath the Riphaean woods, in which he saith Tanais doth first take the beginning. That Donaw (Danubis) doth diuide Asia from Europe, Seneca in the sixth booke Naturall doth manifestly affirm; of which his opinion, what do we thinke, we will, God willing, set downe in the discourse to the Mappe of Dacia. Hitherto we see the forenamed au- thours to doubt and disagree between themselues of the limits of these two parts of the world: if therefore they shall find me a meet vmpier and arbitratour in this matter, I would, not vnfitly, and as I hope to the liking of all parties, decide the controuer thus: I would make the bounds to be Tanais, or the ri- uer Don; the straits or narrow peece of the maine land that is between the riuer and the riuer Rha (Athel), which emptyeth it selfe into the Caspian sea: the East branch of the same Athel: then from his head vnto the riuer Oby, and so even vnto his mouth or fall into the Northren sea: For by this mouth, I do easily perswade my selfe, that antiquity did verily beleeue that the Caspian sea did vnlade it selfe into the maine Ocean. For that the name of this riuer O- by is ancient, it is very likely, for that montes Obij, certain mountaines called Obij are placed heere about in this tract, by Athenaeus, which he saith for- merly were called of the ancient montes Riphæi, the Riphaean hils: but then in his daies montes Alpes, the Alpes. Againe Iornandes in this continent, not farre from hence, describeth Ovim or Obim, a Scythian nation or family. And that these foresaid mountaines are in this place (not where Ptolemey and Pomponius Mela haue placed them) very many men of great credit and learning in these our daies, sufficient witnesses do stoutly auouch. Amongst which Baro Herberftein, in his history of Moscouy, is one: Paul Oderborne, in his treatise written of the life of Bafilides, is another: lastly, Antony Wied, in his mappe of Moscouy may be the third. Now they name it vulgarly by diuers and sundrie names, but commonly they call it Cingulum mundi, The girdle of the world, as the said Herberftein doth affirme. In a mappe of these countries set out by Master Jenkinson, an Englishman, who travelled through these parts, it is called Zona Orbis, The girdle of the Earth. Moreouer I haue in some sort for this diuision, Iornandes and AEthicus, upon my side, where they say that the Riphean mountaines do part Asia and Europe. Againe these selfe same hils, yea, and in this tract, are the montes Hyperbo- rei; not where Ptolemey placeth them. And they are the same with montes Riphæi, Obij, and Alpes. Thus farre of the diusion of Asia from Europe. Pliny calleth this part of the world, The Nurce of all Nations; Mardonius, as Herodotus doth tell of him, auoucheth it to Xerxes, to be by farre the beautifulleft of all places of the World, to be a most goodly and gallant country, yielding all maner of fruits and fruitfull trees and those in their kindes and best; and to be such that it were pity that any king in the World should haue ought to do in but he. Varro in his bo kes Dere Rustica, of Husbandrie writeth, That it is a more temperate and healthful soile than Asia. Statius in his Achilleidos, more than once or twice, calleth it, The Mighty prouince of the World. Maximaterra viris & fœcundissima doctis Vrbibus, Europe for multitude of warlike men and scholers deeply learned, doth farre excell thus Mamilius writeth of it. Aristotle, the prince of Philospophers, maketh the inhabiters of this part of the world, to be, A very stout and couragious people. The same authour affirmeth, that, All kind of beasts and cattell heere, are in their kind, greater, and stronger than in Asia and Africke. But of the nature of this country, the maners and customes of the people, let vs heare what Strabo that excellent Geographer saith in his second book ; This part is most fertile of valiant and prudent men. It is all generally habitable, excepting only a very small portion toward the North, and abutting vpon the Hamaxici, which dwell vpon Tanais, Don Meo'is Palus (Mar delle Zabacche) and Borysthenes (Nieper or Dnester) which place, by reason of the extream cold, is not habitable. Yet certaine bleake and mountainous places inhabited, although in re- spect of the nature of the soile they are tilled and manured with greater difficulty, yet having gotten good skilull and industrious husbands, those also are tamed and much bettered, which heeretofore were badly vsed, and kept only by theeues and out-lawes. And indeed the Greekes when they dwelt vpon the rocks and mountaines, dwelt well and conueniently, by reason of their wise cariage in ciuill matters, arts, sciences and knowledge of those things, which necessarily are required to the maintenance of mans life. In like maner the Romanes, hauing brought many sauage and fierce Nations vnder their seruile yoke, seated, I meane, in places not very conuenient to dwell in, in respect of the nature of the coun- try, either for that it was rough and craggy, or wanted hauens, or was too bleake and cold, or for other causes, taught them to vse merchandise before vnknowen, and haue brought them from a sauage and brutish life, to live ciuilly and more humanely. But those parts which are situate in an equall and temperat climate, there nature administreth all things necessary for the maintenance of man and beast. Now when as those Nations which do inhabite and dwell in fertile and rich countries, are maintainers of peace and quietnesse ; and those which are seated in barren and vn- fruitfull countries, are most hardy and stout : it commeth to passe that both are helpfull one to another ; while these do vse their weapons for their countries defence, those againe do help and maintaine them by the profits that they raise out of the earth, by their arts and mysteries, as also by their learning, wisedome and policy : euen as in like maner also the dammage is mutuall and either side feeleth a sensible hurt, when the one part doth not helpe the other : yet the estate of the souldier and warlike man is somewhat better, if they be not ouercome with multitude. And the nature of Europe serueth very fitly for this purpose : for it is all diuersly distinguished by lofty mountaines and lowly plaines ; so that euery where the husbandmen and soldiers ; the politicians and the martiall warriers do dwell together : yet so as the greatest number are peaceable men : which kind of life they enjoy especially by the meanes and labour of their captaines, first of the Greekes, then of the Macedonians, and lastly of the Romanes. Therefore both in peace and warre it is sufficient of itselfe to maintaine and defend itselfe : for it hath great plenty both of stout soldiers, painfull husbandmen, and politique statesmen. In this also it doth excel, that it bringeth foorth passing good fruits, such I meane, as are necessary for the maintenance of mans life: with all sorts of mettall, for what vse soeuer. Spices or sweet smelling things and preti- ous stones, are brought hither from forren countries, WHICH THINGS WHOSOEVER HAVE NOT, THEY LIVE NEVER A WHIT WORSE THAN THOSE DO THAT HAVE THEM. Moreouer this is especially worth the noting, that hauing wonderfull store of cattell, sheep and oxen ; it breedeth very few dangerous wild beasts. Thus farre the learned Strabo. Barbarie, which is accounted the best, they circumscribe with the Atlantick & Mediterranean seas; with mount Atlas, & whither region of Barca bordering upon Ægypt. Numidia, called by the inhabitants Biledulgerid, and abounding with Dates (for which cause the Arabians call it by no other name, but the Date-bearing region) is bounded Westward by the Atlantick Ocean; Northward by mount Atlas; it stretcheth East as farre as the citie Caphsa, which is an hundred & fiftie miles distant from Ægypt; and the sande Deserts of Libya embrace it on the South. Libya the third part, is named in the Arabian tongue Sura, which word signifies a Desert. It begins East from Nilus, and thence runneth West as farre as the Atlantick sea; Numidia lies to the North of it, and the Land of Negros to the South. Now followeth the fourth part, which they call Nigritarum or Nigritania, either from the inhabitants, who are of a blacke colour, or from the river Niger, that runneth thorow the countrey. It is bounded by Libya, South by the Æthiopick Ocean; West by Guinee, and East by the Kingdome of Gago. And here we are to note, that according unto their division, all Africa is included within the Mediterranean, Atlantick, and Æthiopick seas and the river Nilus; whereof Ægypt and Abassia are accounted parts of it, which we not unworthily therefore proper to belong to Africa. For the true Africa containes at this day Prester Johns Empire, which by all late Writers is ascribed to Africa. Wherefore with Ptolomy indeed it ought to be bound by the Mediterranean sea and Ocean seas, rather than by any river whatsoever; and it hath the forme of a Peninsula, being joined toward Asia by an Isthmos of small neck-land, which lies between the Mediterranean sea and the gulf of Arabia. The South part hereof was unknowne to our ancestors, till the yeere 1497, whereas Vasco de Gama first doubling the Cape of Bona Speranza, and passing about all Africa, came to Calecut in East India. This Southerne part by the Persians and Arabians is called Zangibar. At the fore-sayd Cape of good hope the inhabitants are exceeding blacke; which we thought in no wise to omit, because all men suppose the cause of blacknesse to be heat, and the nearnesse of the Sunne; whereas the Sunne of it selfe forceth no more, than about the Streight of Magellan (we measure the heat of the place, according to the position of the heavens and distance from the Equinoctiall line) were now notwithstanding the people are reported to be marvellous white. But if we will needs ascribe this blacknesse to the scorching of the Sun, let us consider, that the same makes the Spaniards and Italians looke white; whereas they are equally distant from the Equinoctiall with the inhabitants of the fore-sayd Cape; namely the one towards the South, and the other towards the North. Prester Johns people are of a browne colour; in Zanel and Mollala the inhabitants are cole blacke, yet all in one & the same distance from the equator, and under the very same parallell of the heavens. And on the contrary, why did Herodotus and Pindarus describe such as inhabite the same climate with themselves, namely Colches, to be a blacke people, and curled haired? Herodotus in his Thalia makes the Indians blacke like the Æthiopians; which the experience of our times confirmeth. I know Herodotus will have the cause hereof to be the seede of the parents, which he sayth is not whiter, than of other people, but blacke. To whom Possidonius also subscribeth, and imputeth the matter of all this blacknesse unto Canicular heate, against which opinion I have nothing to allege. Let the truth of the matter rest upon the authors credit. But this as many men thinke more strange; that in all America there were no black people found besides a few only in one place called by them Quarequa. What then is the efficient cause of this colour? Is it the drinesse of the heavens, or of the earth? is it perhaps some hidden propriety of the soile? Or a kind of fat licentious nature in the nature of men? Or is it all these variances in one? But these things we leave to be considered by them, who more curiously search into the secrets of nature. This portion of the world is called by the Greeks Libya, and by the Latines Africa, because it is not moistened with extremity of cold: or (if we may credit some) of Afer one of the adventurers posterity. Another derivation of the name you have (set downe by John Leo in the description of his booke of the Description of Africa), who deriveth it from one blacke African, springing of the stocke of Cham, who first inhabited that part of the Mediterranean sea, from Atlas, bounded by the river Nilus, or rather as the best authors have taught us, the African soile called of the Greekes Aphrice, Marce rubrum, and red sea. Herodotus reporteth, that Africa was first unknowne till Cesars time, when it was found to be a third part of the inhabited world. Plinie the like, and so doth Solinus. Caesar Scaliger gathereth of the word Africa, that it is a name of the same import with aprica, that is, a sunne-shining place; for that this land lying open on every side to the Sunnes visitation, seemed thereby to have beene named so little other than of apricity. The principall regions of Africa have amongst themselves (as it were) a threefold division: first, from Atlas in the West to the red sea and Arabia, whence it took the name of the first inhabiters, or else from the colour of the people, for the most part blacke and duskie; the second, to wit, the inner part of Africa, from Nilus to the Kingdome of Congo, is more temperate, and not so much subject to the Sunnes heate; the third, which is next unto the South Pole, by reason of the obliquity of the Zodiack, is more temperate than the former. The islands that belong to Africa (some of which are expressed in the table) are Madagascar, the Canaries, the Isles of Cape Verde, and St. Thomas situated under the equinoctiall, which aboundeth with fruits. Among the ancient Writers none have particularly described this region. But concerning the same saith Isidore in his Etymologies; Hanno his navigation about Africa, in diversities; and Iambulus his voyages, in Diodorus Siculus; and likewise Herodotus his Melpomene. Bartholomeus de Glanvilla in his Image of the World reporteth the like. Of late Writers reade also the Commentaries of Vasco de Gama, Franciscus Alvares, who travelled Æthiopia. But of all others John Leo hath most exactly described it; as also Andrew Thevet, and Francis Alvarez, both travellers of Africa. Concerning the river Nilus, the greatest in all the world, you have the letters of John Ramusio and Jerome Fracastorius.
